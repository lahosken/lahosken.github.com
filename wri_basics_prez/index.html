Audience Creep

Who is the audience?

  • users and devs and... and...
  • everybody who cares about...

Uh-oh. Sounds like audience creep

While your doc is short, then "everybody" is probably an OK answer. But as your doc sprawls, you gotta chunk things up.

Scope Creep for Docs

  • Can't find what you need
  • Can't recognize it
  • Hard to maintain
There's a vicious cycle here. If you're not sure what a doc's purpose is, then you don't know where to add stuff. But if you tack more on to the wrong place, you can muddy the purpose further.

Pause and Reflect when...

Re-organizing documentation is like refactoring code. When you think "I don't know where to add this thing", then re-organize so that it's obvious where to add it.

How to Rein it in?

  • At doc top, 140-character summary
  • Split part into a sub-doc
  • Cross-link between old and new docs
FooBar Design
This document describes the initial design of the FooBar service (which bazzes garplies). It used to also describe the FooBar server configuration, but that moved to another page. Blah blah blah…
FooBar Server Configuration
To configure servers for the FooBar service (which bazzes garplies), you need to understand some fields in a FooBarConfig object. This doc makes more sense if you're familiar with the FooBar service design. Blah blah blah…
A great way to dissuade folks from adding irrelevant stuff to your document is by saying up top what the doc is about. If I don't know what your doc is about, I don't know what's relevant or irrelevant.

Owning a Good Doc

Minimum Docs

  • 140-ch. summ.
  • Good for...
  • Not good for...
  • Contact info
  • Links to more
  • What folks ask
If you write everything you know about some topic, you'll never get anything done. But you want to write enough so that folks know whether they care about your thing. Then they'll ask for other stuff. And when they ask for it, then you know you should write it.

You can get a head start on this. At parties, tell folks about your thing. See what they ask you. If they ask you where to get more beer, you're done writing your document. But if they ask something about your project, remember that.

Someone might have made a template with stuff folks ask about your kind of project. That can be a nice head start. Unless they "stuffed" the template with lots of useless crap.

At the top of any page

  • 140-character topic summary
  • Questions → myteam@wherever.com
  • Links to related documents
You think I'm being an overcute Twitter tweep when I ask for a 140-character summary. But really I'm trying to fool you into writing a summary that I could understand even if I'm an ignoramus who has stumbled onto the wrong document.

How to write high-quality?

  • (a) Perfect the first time* or
  • (b) Peer review

Good Reviewers: Noobs. Experts. Yourself tomorrow morning.

* Yes, that's a joke.

Embarrassed about your English? Ask a peer to review. Surprise: they don't care.

They probably got confused in several places, but it probably doesn't correlate to English problems. But if your sample code doesn't compile? They will get confused.

Handling Suggestions

  • Answers go into Doc
  • Misdirected reader? Link to right Doc
  • N00b in l33t doc? Link to background
Someone asked a question. They are brave! They admitted ignorance. They risked a bikeshed discussion. Now is your chance to call them a moron. Now they're convinced that your thing is unusable and that you're a jerk.

Or be nice but in a way such that you get fewer annoying questions in the future. Answer for a price: price: they have to write that answer into the docs.

"N00b in l33t doc?" You write an advanced doc, which assumes a lot of background knowledge. What do you do when a n00b stumbles in and says you should clutter your doc with all that background? Don't clutter your doc. But do link to places where n00bs can get that background.

"Findability"

Get Folks To Your Doc

  • Put your doc with other docs
  • Try to find it, link from there
  • "Foo HowTo" "Bar PlayBook"
Don't hide it in your Google docs if folks won't look for it there. Google Docs is nice for collaborating, not good for finding.

You group probably has some "usual suspects" document types. It's good if you follow the usual pattern on how you can find such a document. Can I find the Foo PlayBook by going to the main Foo page and the word "playbook" is a link to what I want?
